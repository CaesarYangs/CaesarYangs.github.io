How to learn anything with the Feynman Technique#

Author: Anne-Laure Le Cunff

URL: https://nesslabs.com/feynman-technique

Publication date: September 9, 2019

  • Our current education system is designed to optimise for input. Hours are spent reading, observing, and listening, and output is mostly encouraged as a way to measure the student’s progress. It’s a shame, because there’s lots of research showing that we remember things better when we actively engage with the information and create our own version of it. This is the principle behind the Feynman Technique, named after Richard Feynman (1918–1988), a Nobel-prize winning physicist.
  • Unlike many scientists who mostly use writing to communicate their ideas, Feynman relied heavily on verbal channels, such as talks. Most of his work was transcribed from his lectures. He even dictated most of his books. And this focus on oral communication and teaching others as a way to learn are the basis of the Feynman Technique.

“Without using the new word which you have just learned, try to rephrase what you have just learned in your own language.”–Richard Feynman, Physicist.

1. Pretend to teach the concept to a child. Imagine you’re a middle-school teacher and you need to teach what you’ve just learned to a child. Take a piece of paper and write down everything you know about the subject. Remember your imaginary audience and keep your explanation jargon-free. Don’t hide behind complicated words to mask the gaps in your knowledge. How exactly would you explain this concept to a kid who has no prior knowledge of the subject? Feel free to use doodles to make it easier to grasp. Feynman was a huge fan of visual aids.

2. Fill your knowledge gaps. At some point during this process, you will inevitably get stuck. Perhaps you won’t manage to come up with a simple way to explain part of the concept, or perhaps you’ll realise that an element still feels fuzzy in your mind. That’s perfectly fine: this is actually where the magic happens. Go back to the source material to make sure you understand the concept, and keep on creating your own, simple explanation.

3. Craft a story. Now that you have everything you wanted to learn on your piece of paper, it’s time to turn it into a compelling narrative by organising and simplifying it. Again, think of the imaginary child this explanation is for. Use analogies and comparisons. Read it out loud to see if it flows and to make sure you’re not skipping any steps in the explanation. Once you get to this point, you can be sure you have truly mastered the topic at hand.

“All things are made of atoms — little particles that move around in perpetual motion, attracting each other when they are a little distance apart, but repelling upon being squeezed into one another.”–Richard Feynman, Physicist.

打了双向链接你就会了？ - 少数派#

Author: stonerPAI

URL: https://sspai.com/post/73066

Markdown 早不新鲜了，笔记软件里能插入视频音频也不新鲜了，于是双向链接横空出世了。笔记本摇身一变成了我们的第二大脑了，人类可算进化成双核了。

  • 这两年双向链接功能的笔记软件很火，有些软件更进一步，有脑图(graph view)功能，让用户以点状图的形式看到笔记主题之间的连接。看了之后非常安心，好像打了双向链接，就真的在自己脑袋里连接起来了一样，迷惑性不亚于思维导图。
  • 好在只要用户使用一段时间，让双向链接多起来，再次点开脑图，密密麻麻的点又会告诉你，双向链接没那么神奇，只是你理解错了。

迷思1：笔记软件 = 你的第二大脑

  • 想出这句广告语的人是个天才，简直把笔记本软件拔高了几个档次，以前这种玩意可能叫word或什么，现在是“第二大脑”、“个人wiki”。
  • 脑袋是点状思维，笔记软件使用双向链接，一个page就是一个点。好像笔记本这样模仿脑袋，用户把内容记录上去，链接的越多，掌握的就快。这逻辑通吗？
  • 这完全就是一种错觉。这一过程，打不打双向链接，大脑的记忆模块基本没有任何波澜，只要不经常使用，一个星期准忘。倒是视觉模块，看见点状图，反应会很大，然后用户心理很舒坦。

迷思2：知识是用来管理的

  • 这句话约等于知识是用来存储的。打了标签，有了双链，美其名曰“管理”。然后呢，管理下一个知识吗。不能否认，打标签，相互引用，可以提高查找的效率，但也仅限如此。大多数人还只是在存储。
  • 用以上方式“管理”知识，然后让笔记软件成为第二大脑，其实都暗含一个意思：记录信息，多记录一些，打上标签，给上引用，然后就变成第二个脑袋的知识了，然后有因为软件随处可用，就很容易变成“第一个脑袋”的知识。
  • 从小到大背单词的苦都忘了吗，考试时想不起来知识点的痛还记得吗。知识是那种以某种形式记录下来就能记住的吗。

知识唯有通过使用，才能逐渐内化，没人会反驳这一点，但到了“强大”的笔记软件这里，咋又忘了。我们都很懒惰且贪心，容易上当，学生时代，当有人告诉你有种方法不用背单词就能记住，你一定会去试的。现在，有人告诉你，这个笔记软件很强大，你也一定想去试试。

  • 软件是一种商品，好的设计，用户用的舒心，然后才有销路，这条逻辑完全没错。但是在笔记本软件上，好的设计，用户用的特别舒服，会让用户产生错觉：以为自己记录了，打标签了，给双链了，这就是自己的知识了。这会造成两点：以为自己懂了，可以给别人看了。
  • 这让我回想起学生时代，老师讲课到了重点，会有学生拿起来五颜六色的彩笔和标签纸，老师看到后连忙提醒：先别着急记，学会自己归纳总结。

How Democracy Died in Weimar#

Author: Christopher R. Browning

URL: https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/archive/2022/10/nazi-germany-hitler-democracy-weimar/671605/

Publication date: October 8, 2022

  • Hitler’s lesson from the failed putsch was that he needed to pursue revolution through “the politics of legality” rather than storm Munich City Hall. The Nazis would use the electoral process of democracy to destroy democracy.
  • The next stage of the Nazis’ legal revolution of course had its unique characteristics and circumstances, yet the way Hitler’s faction benefited from the conservative establishment’s support, exploited constitutional vulnerabilities, and undermined political norms to subvert German democracy suggests some portents for American politics today.
  • Hitler soon prevailed on von Hindenburg to use other powers entrusted to the president. In short order, the freedoms of speech, press, and assembly were suspended. An extrajudicial power to arrest and detain people without trial voided normal due process, and this provided a legal basis for the Nazi concentration-camp system. In addition, non-Nazi state governments were deposed, and full legislative powers were vested in the chancellor instead of the Reichstag—in effect allowing rule by fiat. That enabled Hitler to disband labor unions, purge the civil service, and outlaw, one by one, opposing political parties. Within five months, Germany was a one-party dictatorship and a police state.
